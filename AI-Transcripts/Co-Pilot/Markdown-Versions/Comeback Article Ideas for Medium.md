Why I Keep Rebuilding My Projects: The Developer's Curse or Creative Evolution?  

```
Introduction:
If you're a developer like me, you might recognise that familiar feeling
when you revisit a past project or small piece of code and think "I could do this better".
Or maybe, some libraries you used in the past have changed structure slightly,
prompting you to refactor that depreciated code.
Many developers, including myself have revisited and rebuilt past projects before.
This poses a few questions about software development.
Is this constant rebuilding of software projects a flaw, or is it just another feature of being a developer.
Was the code not good enough the first time, or is there always room for improvement,
even if the code seamed 'perfect' at the time?
And is this habit a curse of perfectionism or a sign of creative growth?

Case Study #1: Google BC - My Search Engine That Keeps Evolving
A project that I've come back to a few times is Google BC - my own version of a search engine in Python.
I built it as one of my first projects, back when I was still studying A-level Computer Science and
just used a simple Tkinter interface. The first time I returned to it (towards the end of my A-levels),
I noticed there were 1 or 2 missing features, such as choosing how many results could be shown per page.
After implementing these features, I felt the project had definitely improved.
Then, around a year ago, in October 2024, I decided to revisit it and check it out once again.
However, I discovered that the original API I used for the web search results,
had been deprecated and I would have to use a different one.
I obtained a new API key from the original website where I got the old API from (an API sharing website)
but I had to change some of the methods in my code as the old ones were no longer compatible with my new API.

A screenshot of one of the Google BC Versions from late 2023

Case Study #2: AI Apps Challenge - Reimagining Old AI Projects
In 2024, I completed quite a few personal projects and made a video on my YouTube channel
at the end of the year explaining most of the projects and showcasing them.
A couple of those projects were AI Powered Apps (the AI Cover Letter Writer & AI Testing Agent).
I also made a Work Hours calculator as well as AI Document Searches and an AI Document Extraction Program at work. 
The original non AI-Powered version of the Work Hours Calculator that I made at work and showed during the 2024 Python Projects showcase video

While making that video, I decided that I wanted to do an AI Apps Challenge in 2025,
essentially 12 months of building different AI Powered applications using my Python skills,
newly acquired Flask framework knowledge, and the use of Google Gemini from my Google Cloud Workspace subscription.
I'll be releasing an article talking about that soon, but the first 5 apps were new AI versions of those aforementioned projects.
For the projects that were already AI Powered, this presented the opportunity to do a fresh take on the existing projects and
also build personal versions of projects I had already built at work.
For the work hours calculator, I was especially excited to see how the AI Version would turn out, and it didn't disappoint.
More on these projects coming soon in January 2026, make sure you subscribe to get notified when I post.
AI Version of the Work Hours calculator as part of AI Apps Challenge 2025

The Mini Project Habit: Why I Keep Tweaking
Recently, I've been returning to quite a few mini-projects or small scripts,
reviewing them, making slight tweaks and exploring them again.
For example, I've edited a few small tools, such as directory listers, duplicate line removers, logger interfaces and more.
Most of the edits are just small tweaks, amendments to past design decisions or commenting out some redundant code.
But also, sometimes I think of new features that could be added, were missing or have recently come out,
especially with some small AI powered tools.
This mostly stems from the fast evolution of technology or deprecation of some libaries/frameworks over time.
Most of the time, however, curiosity fuels my exploring of past projects and sometimes editing them.
I also enjoy making the small refinements and especially adding new features, which does take longer,
but is usually more satisfying when I see them in action.

The Psychology Behind Rebuilding
The main reason for rebuilding, I believe comes down to evolving technologies and thinking of new ideas.
Often it can also be because you learnt something new and when revisiting an old project,
thought of how it could apply there and wanted to put it into practice.
It definitely does play into the growth mindset of a developer and how old projects can feel outdated quite quickly.
That being said though, improving old projects can be a time-consuming job sometimes,
especially if that project is something that's never gonna be shown to others or actually used for real-world problems.
On the other hand, applying new concepts to existing projects can be an excellent way to enhance learning and build developer experience.
For me, usually it's a mix of both.
Sometimes, I feel like I'm making real progress in my learning journey and that making the modifications has really helped me
to understand a concept better or develop my programming skills.
Other times however, I feel like I just wasted my time doing something there wasn't much point doing and every hour
I spend trying to make that time worthwhile won't amount to anything much in the long run.

Modifying Existing Projects vs. Working on new ones: What I've Learned
Personally, I believe modifying existing projects is more worthwhile if you know your skills are being developed,
a new concept is being applied for the first time or that the project will actually be used a decent amount - either by yourself,
other developers or general users. My advice to fellow developers is to mostly work on current projects or start new ones,
but do revisit old projects every once in a while and see if there's any immediate room for improvement.
Sometimes code you wrote in the past can also be useful for current and/or future projects and you may discover a few hidden gems hidden within past projects.

Conclusion: The Evolution Continues
So back to the original question - is it a curse or is the constant evolution of software projects a feature for most developers?
As stated before, the answer isn't quite as straight forward or simple.
Usually it can depend on the face value of the project you're revisiting.
But sometimes, there can be hidden value beyond the surface which you only discover later on - and that's good as well.
Remember, rebuilding projects isn't regression and usually isn't a waste of time - it's refinement and helps build your skills as a developer.
```  
