```
John Ball is a cognitive scientist, computer engineer and author. In his new book, ‘How to Solve AI with Our Brain’ published in November 2024, John talks about his vision to bring trustworthy language AI to the world. Since 2006, he has been refining software that recognises human languages.

After a time working on language-to-meaning representation, it became clear that something was lacking, and progress was being held back. Then, the missing piece was discovered: the unexploited linguistic framework called Role and Reference Grammar.

"I have implemented it on a machine to demonstrate human-like understanding and generation of language. Even though human babies learn their mother tongue, ‘understanding’ by machines remains elusive for even today’s most powerful generative AI models".

John will be joined by Beth Carey, the co-founder and director of Language Parent, with over 20 years of experience in corporate technology, she has led large teams of technologists, established and exited businesses, and speaks on AI topics at academic and industry symposiums.

John’s corporate IT career was underpinned by his BS in computer science, mathematics, physics and engineering and an MS in Cognitive Science. Starting at IBM, he held national-level technical roles managing their largest mainframe computers
```

🧠✨ Last week I had the privilege of attending the *Trustworthy AI* event at the University of Bradford, featuring cognitive scientist and author John Ball, joined by Beth Carey of Language Parent.

The session was a deep dive into the limitations of today’s statistical AI models—and a bold vision for what comes next.

🔍 John’s central thesis? That true language understanding in machines won’t come from more data or bigger models, but from *natural science*—specifically brain science and linguistics. His work with Patom Theory and Role and Reference Grammar (RRG) proposes a meaning-first approach to AI, where context, cognition, and human-like reasoning take center stage.

- AI today is unsustainable—computationally, ethically, and environmentally.
- Language isn’t just word statistics. It’s layered, contextual, and deeply tied to how humans perceive and interact.

- Brain science is the final frontier—machines don’t “understand” like humans do, and that gap matters.
- Collaboration between neuroscientists, linguists, and ML engineers is essential to move beyond current limitations.
